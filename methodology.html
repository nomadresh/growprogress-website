The Problem
Most research tells you what people think. It can't tell you what will change their minds.

Traditional polling captures a snapshot of existing opinion. A/B tests tell you which message got more clicks from people who were already inclined to engage. Neither answers the question campaigns actually need answered: does this message persuade someone who doesn't already agree with me?

That distinction matters enormously. A message that performs well with your base can actively backfire with persuadable voters. A frame that tests well in a survey can fail in the field. Without a control group, you can never know the difference between a message that converted someone and an audience that was already converted.

Grow Progress was built to answer that question — rigorously, quickly, and at a cost campaigns and nonprofits can actually afford.

01
The Sample

A nationally representative panel, recruited for your study.

We field each study with a nationally representative panel of U.S. adults, recruited through a network of high-quality research partners. Demographic quotas ensure the sample reflects the population you care about — whether that's likely voters in a swing state, parents of school-age children, or members of a specific union. You define the audience; we field to it.

02
Quality Controls

Every respondent passes the same quality threshold.

Low-quality responses are screened out before they reach your data. We apply attention checks, speeder traps, and consistency filters to every study. Respondents who fail are replaced, not averaged in. The result is a clean sample — one you can trust to reflect genuine opinion, not noise.

03
Random Assignment

Respondents are randomly split into two groups.

Half the sample is assigned to the control group; half to the test group. The assignment is random. This is the foundation of causal inference — it means the two groups are statistically identical before the experiment begins, differing only in what they're about to see.

Why this matters: Without random assignment, you can't isolate the effect of your message. Any difference you observe might be explained by differences between the groups, not by the message itself. Random assignment eliminates that ambiguity.

04
One Difference

The control group sees neutral content. The test group sees your message.

Both groups complete the same survey experience — with one exception. The test group is exposed to your message, ad, mail piece, or script. The control group sees a neutral filler stimulus on an unrelated topic. Everything else — the survey flow, question wording, response options — is identical.

05
The Success Question

Both groups answer the same question about your issue.

After the exposure, both groups are asked the same question — typically a measure of support, favorability, or intended action on your issue. We call this the success question. The difference in responses between the two groups is what we're measuring. The control group gives us the baseline. The test group shows us whether the message moved the needle.

06
Measuring Response

We compare the baseline to the test group's responses.

The gap between the control group's answer and the test group's answer is the raw effect of your message. A positive gap means your message moved people in the direction you intended. A negative gap means it backfired. No gap means it had no measurable effect. All three outcomes are valuable — they tell you something different about how your message is landing.

07
Weighting

Results are weighted to reflect your true target population.

Survey samples rarely match the real world perfectly. We apply raking weights to align the sample to known population benchmarks — age, gender, race, education, geography, and more. This ensures the lift score you see reflects the actual population you're trying to reach, not an artifact of who happened to respond to your survey.

08
The Lift Score

The gap becomes your Grow Progress Lift — with confidence intervals.

The weighted difference between the test and control groups is your Lift score. We report it alongside confidence intervals and statistical significance markers, so you always know how much to trust the result. A lift score without confidence intervals is incomplete; ours show you not just what happened, but how certain we are that it's real.

What Lift means in practice: A +8 point lift means that among people who saw your message, 8 more out of every 100 moved in the direction you intended, compared to people who saw nothing. That's causal movement — not correlation, not engagement, not preference among existing supporters.

09
Subgroup Analysis

Every study breaks down results across the audiences that matter to you.

Overall lift tells you whether a message works on average. Subgroup analysis tells you for whom. Every study includes breakdowns by age, gender, race, education, household income, parental status, party ID, political ideology, geography, and more. A message that generates a +12 lift with persuadable independents may show no effect — or even backlash — among base voters. Knowing the difference is what allows you to target with precision.
