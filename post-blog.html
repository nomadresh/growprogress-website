Why focus groups keep failing campaigns, and what to do instead

Focus groups measure attention and preference. They don't measure persuasion. Here's why that distinction matters and what a randomized controlled trial actually tells you.

The core problem with focus groups

Every campaign strategist has been in that room. A dozen voters gathered around a conference table in a hotel ballroom, a moderator asking what they think about an ad, a one-way mirror showing the client watching from the dark. The conversation is animated. People have opinions. The video clips back beautifully. And then the ad runs, and moves nobody.

This is not a coincidence. Focus groups are structurally incapable of measuring the thing campaigns actually need to know: whether a message changes minds. They were designed to surface vocabulary, surface-level reactions, and creative direction feedback. That's genuinely useful for some things. It is not useful for predicting persuasive impact.

The fundamental issue is self-report. When you ask people what they think about a message in a social setting, they tell you what they want to think they think, filtered through social desirability, group dynamics, and the particular personalities in that room. The person who dominates the conversation shapes the output. The introvert who quietly changed her mind goes unnoticed. You get a performance of opinion, not a measurement of it.

What persuasion actually requires

Persuasion is a causal claim. You're asserting that exposing someone to your message causes a shift in their belief, intention, or behavior. Causal claims require a counterfactual: what would that same person have thought if they seen your message?

Focus groups have no counterfactual. Every participant saw the message. There's no baseline, no control group, no way to isolate the message's effect from the moderator's framing, the room's temperature, or who laughed first. You're measuring reaction, not change.

This distinction, reaction versus change, is the entire game. A message can produce strong reaction and zero persuasion. We see this constantly. The ad that generates passionate focus group discussion often turns out to be activating, not converting: it excites people who already agree while doing nothing for the persuadable middle. The quiet, slightly boring ad that nobody raves about turns out to move the needle by 14 points.

  1. Reaction is visible and social; persuasion is private and causal.
  2. Qualitative feedback optimizes for what sounds right; RCTs optimize for what works.
  3. Group dynamics systematically amplify certain voices and suppress others.
  4. Without a control group, you cannot know what "no message" would have produced.

How RCTs solve it

A randomized controlled trial assigns people at random to either see your message (treatment) or not (control). You then measure the outcome you care about, a policy support question, a candidate favorability rating, a stated vote intention, in both groups. The difference is the causal effect of your message.

Because assignment is random, the two groups are equivalent on every other dimension: demographics, prior beliefs, news consumption, the weather that day. Any difference in outcomes can be attributed to the message and nothing else. That's the counterfactual you can't get from a focus group.

You can know, with statistical confidence, which of three messages moves your target audience most, and by how much, before you've spent a dollar on media.

"You don't need to know if people like your ad. You need to know if it changed their mind."

What Grow Progress's data shows

Across 500+ message tests, a consistent pattern emerges: the message that wins in focus group discussion is not reliably the message that wins in a randomized test. In fact, focus group participants correctly predicted the RCT winner in fewer than half of the tests where we had both data sources. That's worse than a coin flip.

The messages that resonate in a facilitated group setting tend to be vivid, memorable, and emotionally charged, qualities that make for good conversation. But persuasion in the real world is quieter. The most effective messages are often the ones that give a skeptical person a plausible reason to update, not the ones that generate applause from people who already agree.

When to use each

This is not an argument that focus groups are worthless. They're excellent for specific, limited purposes, early-stage creative exploration, vocabulary research, understanding how an audience frames an issue in their own words. If you're trying to figure out what language a particular community uses to talk about healthcare, a focus group gives you that quickly and richly.

The mistake is using focus groups to answer persuasion questions. "Which of these messages will move the most voters?" is not a focus group question. It's an RCT question. The methods are designed for different problems, and conflating them costs campaigns real money and real votes.

A practical division: use qualitative research to generate hypotheses and vocabulary, then use RCTs to test which hypotheses actually hold. Focus groups feed the creative brief; RCTs grade it. Both have a role. Neither replaces the other, but only one of them can tell you what actually changes minds.

The campaigns that consistently outperform aren't the ones with the best creative instincts. They're the ones that test, learn, and iterate on causal evidence rather than group consensus. That infrastructure, fast, rigorous, connected to real outcomes, is what separates guessing from knowing.
