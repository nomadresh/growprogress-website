New Research Shows Even Trump Voters Are Rethinking ICE

KG
Kunal Goel
February 10, 2026
ICE

In the weeks following the murders of Renee Good and Alex Pretti, both killed by individuals with prior immigration enforcement encounters, Grow Progress mobilized with partners at the American Immigration Council (AIC) and the International Refugee Assistance Project (IRAP) to understand whether the dominant enforcement narrative could be shifted, even among Trump voters. The results were striking: across the political spectrum, exposure to information about ICE's conduct significantly increased disapproval, and the effects were largest among the groups typically assumed to be most resistant.

Republican voters are changing their minds about ICE, Trump's handling of immigration

Across all eight messages tested, the average treatment effect on ICE disapproval was statistically significant. Trump voters moved +18 points in disapproval of ICE after exposure to tested messages. Republicans moved +19 points. Independent voters, a critical persuadable bloc, moved +20 points. These are not marginal shifts. In a research context where a 3–5 point effect is typically considered meaningful, movements of this magnitude signal genuine opinion fluidity on what had appeared to be a settled political issue.

"This research confirms what we hear from our clients every day: people are questioning what's happening at the border and in their communities. When they learn the facts about how ICE is operating, separating families, detaining people with no criminal history, making errors that harm citizens, their views shift. This data gives us a roadmap for reaching people we thought were unreachable." , Henrike Dessaules, Senior Director of Communications, IRAP
"For too long, the immigration debate has been framed as enforcement vs. open borders. This research shows there's a third frame that resonates broadly: safety, accountability, and basic human dignity. Those values don't belong to one party. When we communicate in those terms, we reach people across the political spectrum, including Trump voters." , Kimberly Serrano, Director of the Center for Inclusion and Belonging, American Immigration Council

Respondents prioritize public safety, but want Trump and ICE to focus on minimizing community harm

The data revealed a consistent pattern: respondents across partisan groups said they support immigration enforcement focused on individuals who pose genuine public safety threats, but they draw a sharp line at enforcement practices that harm communities, detain people without criminal records, or create conditions for errors with serious consequences. Messages that acknowledged enforcement as legitimate while highlighting its current overreach and lack of accountability were the most effective at shifting opinions. The frame of "ICE is failing at its own stated mission" proved particularly persuasive, including with Republican respondents who did not respond to messaging that criticized enforcement philosophically.

"Abolish ICE" messaging gained some traction, but middle ground approaches also proved persuasive

One of the notable findings was that messaging explicitly calling for abolishing ICE performed meaningfully better than researchers expected, particularly among younger voters and voters of color. However, messages focused on reforming ICE, increasing accountability, and refocusing the agency on genuine threats outperformed abolition messaging across the full sample by 6–9 percentage points. The implication is not that abolition framing is ineffective, but that reform and accountability frames have broader persuasive reach, particularly with the persuadable center that will determine most contested electoral and policy fights.

Views on Trump and aggressive ICE tactics are changing for a number of demographics

The subgroup data showed significant movement beyond the partisan splits. College-educated suburban voters, a key swing constituency, moved +22 points on ICE disapproval. Women under 50 moved +24 points. Latino voters, who are sometimes assumed to be a monolithic pro-enforcement constituency, showed +17 point shifts, with particularly strong movement on messages connecting ICE tactics to harm in immigrant communities. Even among self-identified conservatives, 4 of the 8 messages produced statistically significant effects, suggesting that the ceiling on persuasion in this space is higher than conventional political wisdom has assumed.

Next Steps

The research points toward a clear communications strategy: lead with public safety and accountability framing, use specific examples of ICE overreach that cut across ideological lines, and avoid messaging that frames immigration enforcement as inherently illegitimate. For AIC, IRAP, and allied organizations, this data provides a tested foundation for campaigns across earned media, paid advertising, and grassroots organizing. The next phase of research will test video executions of the highest-performing message frames and explore subgroup-specific tailoring for key constituencies in states with contested 2026 Senate and House races.
